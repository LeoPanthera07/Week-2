PART 1 — RAW DATA EXPLORATION

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Q1: Is age linearly related to survival? Justify statistically.
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANSWER: NO — Age is NOT linearly related to survival.

STATISTICAL JUSTIFICATION:

1. Pearson Correlation Coefficient:
   r = -0.0777 (computed using np.corrcoef())

   Interpretation: This indicates a NEGLIGIBLE negative linear relationship.

2. Coefficient of Determination:
   R² = r² = 0.006 (0.6%)

   Meaning: Only 0.6% of the variance in survival can be explained by age
   through a linear model. This is essentially zero predictive power.

3. Correlation Strength Classification:
   |r| < 0.1 → Negligible
   0.1 ≤ |r| < 0.3 → Weak
   0.3 ≤ |r| < 0.5 → Moderate
   |r| ≥ 0.5 → Strong

   Our |r| = 0.078 falls in the "negligible" category.

4. Non-Linear Patterns Observed:
   • Children (< 15): 59.4% survival rate
   • Adults (15-60): 38.9% survival rate  
   • Seniors (> 60): 22.7% survival rate

   This U-shaped pattern (children prioritized, seniors disadvantaged) cannot
   be captured by linear correlation. The relationship is NON-LINEAR.

BUSINESS INTERPRETATION:

Age alone is a poor linear predictor of survival. The "women and children 
first" protocol created a non-linear advantage for young passengers, while 
elderly passengers faced mobility constraints during evacuation. Any survival 
model should use AGE GROUPS (categorical) rather than raw age (continuous).

Q2: Does wealth dominate gender in predicting survival?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANSWER: NO — Gender DOMINATES wealth in predicting survival.

STATISTICAL EVIDENCE:

1. Cross-Tabulation Analysis (Sex × FareGroup):

   Survival Rates:

   | Gender | Low Fare | Medium Fare | High Fare |
   |--------|----------|-------------|-----------|
   | Female | 49.0%    | 88.5%       | 96.5%     |
   | Male   | 11.5%    | 21.8%       | 37.5%     |

2. Critical Comparison:

   • Low-fare FEMALES: 49.0% survival
   • High-fare MALES:  37.5% survival

   Gap: +11.5 percentage points in favor of low-fare females

   Even the POOREST women survived at a higher rate than the RICHEST men.

3. Correlation Coefficients with Survival:

   • Sex_binary (female): r = +0.543 (STRONG positive)
   • Fare:                r = +0.257 (WEAK positive)
   • Pclass:              r = -0.338 (MODERATE negative)

   Gender has 2.1× stronger correlation than fare with survival.

4. Within-Gender Patterns:

   Among FEMALES:
   • Low fare → 49% survival
   • High fare → 96.5% survival
   • Wealth matters significantly (+47.5 points)

   Among MALES:
   • Low fare → 11.5% survival
   • High fare → 37.5% survival  
   • Wealth matters moderately (+26 points)

   BUT the poorest woman still beats the richest man by 11.5 points.

CONCLUSION:

The "women and children first" evacuation protocol completely overrode 
economic privilege for male passengers. Gender was the single strongest 
predictor of survival. Wealth only mattered WITHIN gender groups — it could 
not overcome the primary sorting criterion of sex.

This reflects the strict enforcement of social norms during the disaster, 
where chivalry/honor codes trumped class-based advantages for men.

Q3: Does your handcrafted score outperform random guessing significantly?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANSWER: YES — The handcrafted scoring rule significantly outperforms random 
        guessing and naive baselines.

PERFORMANCE METRICS:

1. Model Accuracy: 78.9%

   Confusion Matrix:

                    Predicted: 0   Predicted: 1
   Actual: 0 (died)      458            91
   Actual: 1 (survived)   97           245

   • True Positives:  245
   • True Negatives:  458
   • False Positives: 91
   • False Negatives: 97

2. Additional Metrics:

   • Precision: 72.9% (of predicted survivors, 72.9% were correct)
   • Recall:    71.6% (caught 71.6% of actual survivors)
   • F1 Score:  0.723 (balanced precision/recall)

3. Baseline Comparisons:

   • Random guessing (50/50):        50.0%
   • Majority class (always "died"): 61.6%
   • Our handcrafted model:          78.9%

   Improvement over random:    +28.9 percentage points (1.58× better)
   Improvement over majority:  +17.3 percentage points

STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE:

Using a simple proportion test:

Model vs Random (50%):
  z = (0.789 - 0.50) / sqrt(0.5 * 0.5 / 891) = 17.24
  p-value < 0.0001 (HIGHLY significant)

Model vs Majority (61.6%):
  z = (0.789 - 0.616) / sqrt(0.616 * 0.384 / 891) = 10.62  
  p-value < 0.0001 (HIGHLY significant)

Both comparisons yield p < 0.0001, meaning the probability of achieving this
performance by chance is less than 0.01%. The result is STATISTICALLY 
SIGNIFICANT at any reasonable confidence level.

INTERPRETATION:

The handcrafted weighted scoring formula, built purely from correlation 
analysis (no ML algorithms), provides substantial predictive power:

• 78.9% accuracy vs 50% random → 58% relative improvement
• Beats naive majority baseline by 17.3 points
• F1 score of 0.723 shows balanced performance (not just accuracy gaming)

The model works because it correctly identifies and weights the strongest 
survival predictors:
  1. Gender (50% weight) — strongest signal
  2. Passenger class (25% weight) — proxy for lifeboat access
  3. Fare (15% weight) — individual spending power
  4. Age group (8% weight) — vulnerability/priority
  5. Family size (2% weight) — weak but included

CONCLUSION:

YES — the handcrafted score outperforms random guessing with overwhelming 
statistical significance (p < 0.0001). It demonstrates that transparent, 
interpretable feature engineering can achieve strong predictive performance 
without black-box ML models.

PART 4 — EXECUTIVE CHALLENGE

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Q4: If Titanic happened today, who should be prioritized for rescue?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANSWER: Modern rescue prioritization should focus on VULNERABILITY, not 
        gender or wealth.

PROPOSED PRIORITY ORDER:

1. CHILDREN (< 15 years)
   Rationale: 
   • Limited self-rescue capability
   • Highest vulnerability
   • Long potential life-years saved
   Historical survival: 59.4%

2. ELDERLY (> 60 years)  
   Rationale:
   • Physical mobility constraints
   • Medical needs
   • Historically had LOWEST survival (22.7%) — must correct this

3. PERSONS with DISABILITIES
   Rationale:
   • May require assistance
   • Slower evacuation speed
   • Cannot self-rescue effectively

4. FAMILIES with YOUNG CHILDREN
   Rationale:
   • Children require adult supervision
   • Keep family units together for psychological stability
   • Parent separation causes trauma

5. REMAINING PASSENGERS (random/first-come-first-served)
   Rationale:
   • No gender-based discrimination (violates modern equality norms)
   • No wealth-based discrimination (violates fairness principles)

KEY DIFFERENCES FROM 1912:

Remove:
  ✗ Gender as priority factor ("women first" = gender discrimination)
  ✗ Wealth/class advantages (equal treatment regardless of ticket price)

Add:
  ✓ Disability status (ADA/accessibility requirements)
  ✓ Medical conditions (triage based on health needs)
  ✓ Pregnancy status (counted as vulnerable category)

Modern ethical frameworks prioritize:
  • Vulnerability > Gender
  • Need > Wealth  
  • Equality > Chivalry

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Q5: Identify 3 ethical concerns in automated survival prediction.
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANSWER: Three critical ethical concerns with automated survival prediction:

1. DISCRIMINATION & ALGORITHMIC BIAS

   Problem:
   • Historical data encodes societal biases (gender, race, class)
   • Models trained on biased data perpetuate discrimination
   • Legally protected groups may be systematically de-prioritized

   Example from Our Model:
   • Our model assigns 50% weight to gender (female = higher survival)
   • This was learned from 1912 social norms, not biological necessity
   • Automating this creates gender discrimination in rescue decisions

   Real-World Impact:
   • Protected characteristics (gender, race, age) used for life-death decisions
   • Violates equal protection laws in many jurisdictions
   • Creates disparate impact on minorities

   Legal Framework Violated:
   • Title VII (Civil Rights Act) — employment discrimination
   • Equal Protection Clause (14th Amendment) — government discrimination
   • GDPR Article 22 — right to explanation for automated decisions

2. OPACITY & ACCOUNTABILITY GAP

   Problem:
   • Black-box models make uninterpretable decisions
   • No clear responsibility chain when algorithm causes harm
   • Families cannot understand why loved ones were not saved

   Accountability Questions:
   • Who is liable? Data scientist? Company? Algorithm? Captain?
   • Can the decision be appealed or overridden?
   • How do we audit the decision post-disaster?

   Example Scenario:
   A passenger with "survival score 0.35" is denied lifeboat access and dies.
   Post-disaster investigation reveals:
   • The model used outdated training data
   • A bug in the scoring function reduced their score by 0.2
   • The passenger had a medical condition not captured in the model

   Who is responsible for this death? Current legal frameworks have no answer.

   Transparency Requirements:
   • GDPR: Right to explanation for automated decisions
   • But explanations of complex models are often post-hoc rationalizations
   • True interpretability requires sacrificing accuracy (tradeoff)

3. DEHUMANIZATION & TRIAGE ETHICS

   Problem:
   • Reducing human life to a numerical score treats people as statistics
   • Ignores intrinsic human dignity, context, and randomness
   • Removes empathy, judgment, and moral reasoning from decisions

   Philosophical Issues:

   a) Utilitarianism vs Deontology:
      • Algorithm optimizes for "most lives saved" (utilitarian)
      • But some lives might have more "value"? (controversial)
      • Deontological ethics: humans have intrinsic worth, not comparable

   b) The Trolley Problem:
      • Is algorithmic triage equivalent to actively choosing who dies?
      • Differs from random/first-come-first-served (no active choice)

   c) Context Blindness:
      Algorithm cannot consider:
      • Pregnant woman (not visible in data)
      • Parent of orphaned children (family context)
      • Doctor who could save others post-rescue (social utility)
      • Person's life achievements, relationships, meaning

   Example:
   • Passenger A (young, wealthy): Score 0.75
   • Passenger B (elderly, poor): Score 0.25

   Should we save A and abandon B? What if:
   • B is a renowned surgeon who could treat other survivors
   • B has young grandchildren dependent on them
   • A has no dependents and no critical skills

   The algorithm sees none of this context.

   Human Dignity Argument:
   • Every human has equal intrinsic worth (Kantian ethics)
   • Scoring people violates the categorical imperative
   • "Never treat humanity as means only, but always as end in itself"

CONCLUSION:

Automated survival prediction systems create a trilemma:
  • Accuracy → Often requires opaque models → Accountability problems
  • Transparency → Requires simple models → Lower accuracy → More deaths
  • Fairness → Requires removing protected features → May reduce accuracy

There is no technical solution to this ethical problem. It requires:
  • Regulation (what models are allowed)
  • Oversight (human-in-the-loop)  
  • Transparency (explainable decisions)
  • Accountability (clear liability)

Q6: If this were insurance underwriting, how would your logic change?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ANSWER: Insurance underwriting would require fundamental logic changes due to 
        regulatory, temporal, and adversarial constraints.

CURRENT MODEL (Disaster Survival Prediction):

Purpose:  Maximize survival probability for rescue prioritization
Output:   Binary classification (survive / die)
Horizon:  Immediate (hours)
Optimize: Maximize recall (save as many as possible)
Features: Age, gender, class, fare (from disaster context)
Legal:    No constraints (emergency context)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

INSURANCE MODEL (Risk Assessment & Pricing):

Purpose:  Estimate risk of claim (death/injury) to set premium prices
Output:   Continuous risk score → premium amount (INR/USD per year)
Horizon:  Long-term (decades)
Optimize: Maximize profit (balance risk vs premium, avoid adverse selection)
Features: Actuarial risk factors (health, lifestyle, occupation)
Legal:    HEAVILY regulated (anti-discrimination laws)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KEY CHANGES REQUIRED:

1. INVERTED INTERPRETATION

   Disaster Model:
   • High survival score → prioritize for rescue
   • Low survival score → lower priority

   Insurance Model:  
   • High survival score → LOW risk → LOW premium
   • Low survival score → HIGH risk → HIGH premium

   Same model, opposite business logic.

2. REGULATORY COMPLIANCE (Legal Constraints)

   PROHIBITED FEATURES (varies by jurisdiction):

   United States:
   • Gender-based pricing: BANNED in some states (Montana, California)
   • Age discrimination: Allowed but regulated (Age Discrimination Act)
   • Race, religion: PROHIBITED (Title VII)
   • Genetic information: PROHIBITED (GINA — Genetic Information 
                                      Nondiscrimination Act, 2008)

   European Union:
   • Gender-based pricing: BANNED (EU Gender Directive 2012)
   • Age-based pricing: Heavily regulated
   • GDPR restrictions on automated decision-making (Article 22)

   India:
   • IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory Authority) prohibits discrimination
   • Gender-based pricing: Restricted
   • Age: Allowed within limits

   Our Titanic Model VIOLATES These Laws:
   • Uses gender as 50% of weight → ILLEGAL in EU, some US states
   • Uses raw age without medical justification → problematic
   • No health data → cannot justify risk assessment

3. FEATURE ENGINEERING OVERHAUL

   REMOVE (illegal/irrelevant for long-term risk):
   ✗ Gender (illegal in many jurisdictions)
   ✗ Passenger class / Socioeconomic status (discriminatory)
   ✗ Fare / Ticket price (not predictive of long-term mortality)
   ✗ Embarkation port (irrelevant)

   ADD (actuarial risk factors):
   ✓ Health history (pre-existing conditions, surgeries, medications)
   ✓ Family medical history (hereditary diseases)
   ✓ Lifestyle factors:
     - Smoking status (yes/no, packs per day)
     - Alcohol consumption (units per week)
     - Exercise frequency (hours per week)
     - BMI / obesity status
   ✓ Occupation (hazardous jobs → higher risk)
   ✓ Driving record (accidents, violations)
   ✓ Geographic location (pollution, crime rates, healthcare access)
   ✓ Genetic markers (if legal and consented)

4. TEMPORAL MODELING (Long-Term Risk)

   Disaster model: Predicts immediate outcome (next 2 hours)
   Insurance model: Predicts risk over policy lifetime (10-30 years)

   Requires:
   • Age-based mortality curves (actuarial life tables)
   • Time-varying covariates (health deteriorates with age)
   • Cohort effects (generation-specific risk profiles)
   • Survival analysis models (Cox proportional hazards, Kaplan-Meier)

   Example:
   • 25-year-old: Low immediate risk, but 30-year horizon
   • 65-year-old: Higher immediate risk, shorter horizon
   • Premium must account for both current risk AND time horizon

5. ADVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS (Fraud Prevention)

   Disaster scenario: No incentive to lie (want to be rescued)
   Insurance scenario: Strong incentive to lie (lower premiums)

   Adversarial Behaviors:
   • Underreport smoking, alcohol use
   • Hide pre-existing conditions
   • Falsify health records
   • Apply for insurance when already sick (adverse selection)

   Countermeasures Needed:
   • Medical exams (blood tests, BMI measurement)
   • Third-party health record verification
   • Anomaly detection (statistical outliers flagged)
   • Waiting periods (pre-existing condition exclusions)
   • Claim investigation teams

6. BUSINESS OPTIMIZATION (Profit vs Risk)

   Disaster model: Minimize false negatives (don't leave survivors behind)
   Insurance model: Balance false positives/negatives for profit

   Insurance Optimization:

   Profit = Premium Revenue - Expected Claims - Operating Costs

   Must avoid:
   • Underpricing (lose money on claims)
   • Overpricing (lose customers to competitors)
   • Adverse selection (only high-risk people buy insurance)

   Risk Management:
   • Reinsurance (transfer extreme risk to reinsurers)
   • Portfolio diversification (mix of low/high risk customers)
   • Reserve requirements (regulatory capital for claims)

7. EXPLAINABILITY REQUIREMENTS

   GDPR Article 22: Right to explanation for automated decisions

   Insurance customers can request:
   • "Why is my premium $X?"
   • "What factors increased my risk score?"
   • "If I quit smoking, how much will premium decrease?"

   Model must provide:
   • Feature importance (which factors matter most)
   • Counterfactual explanations ("if you change X, premium becomes Y")
   • Human-interpretable reasoning

   Black-box neural networks are PROBLEMATIC for insurance.
   Prefer: Linear models, decision trees, GAMs (Generalized Additive Models)

REVISED INSURANCE MODEL ARCHITECTURE:

Input Features (Legally Compliant):
  • Age (allowed with medical justification)
  • BMI, blood pressure, cholesterol (objective health metrics)
  • Smoking status (verified via medical exam)
  • Occupation risk category (actuarial tables)
  • Family history of specific diseases (heart disease, cancer, diabetes)
  • Exercise frequency (self-reported but correlated with claims)
  • Location (zip code level, not individual)

Prohibited Features:
  ✗ Gender (in EU, some US states)
  ✗ Race / ethnicity
  ✗ Religion
  ✗ Genetic test results (GINA in US)
  ✗ Socioeconomic status / income / wealth

Model Type:
  • Generalized Linear Model (GLM) or Generalized Additive Model (GAM)
  • Transparent, explainable, auditable
  • Coefficients justified by actuarial science

Output:
  • Annual mortality risk probability
  • Premium = Base Rate × Risk Multiplier × Policy Coverage

Example:
  Base Rate: $1000/year (population average)
  Risk Multiplier: 1.35 (smoker, age 45, high BMI)
  Premium: $1,350/year

Validation:
  • Backtesting on historical claims data
  • Actuarial review by certified actuaries
  • Regulatory approval (IRDAI in India, NAIC in US)
